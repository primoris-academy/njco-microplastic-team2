Why Plastics Are So Durable


All plastics are polymers, so they are all very durable. A polymer is a chain hundreds of thousands of monomers long. Carbon, since it can bond to other carbons as well as other atoms often forms polymers. The polymer chains that form plastics give attributes not found in other natural materials. These chains are very long, ensuring that the polymers are strong, water-resistant, and flexible.

Chemicals mixed in with the base polymer of plastics ensure that the polymer is not biodegradable and attributes it unnatural properties. Antioxidants prevent the polymer from degrading, plasticizers improve the flexibility, and UV protectors are also added. Adding carbon, glass, and fibers and compounding the materials can make the plastic fire-retardant.

An example are polycarbonates: a very durable thermoplastic polymer

While the lightweight, water and decay resistant properties of plastic are valued during use, they have a negative effect on the environment. The world's waters and land are being swamped in plastic that takes years to decompose while plastic-softening agents have a harmful effect on the environment. Bacteria and fungi are not able to break down plastic except in rare, spread out occurrences.
